## 1. Purpose

This document translates the preliminary NormTrace-IHR mapping of Mexican domestic law against the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) into a set of structured entry points for institutional dialogue, technical assistance, legislative review, and capacity-building support. It draws on the Mexico–IHR 2005 mapping table, the implementation gap map, and the legal internalisation snapshot produced in earlier NormTrace outputs.

The document is addressed to international organisations (WHO, PAHO, IPU, UNITE), policy labs, national delegations, parliamentary bodies, and technical experts engaged in pandemic governance, global health law, and IHR implementation support. It is not addressed to litigation or judicial processes.

**This document does not:** declare, assess, or imply compliance or non-compliance with any international instrument. It does not constitute legal advice. It does not propose definitive legislative reforms. It identifies areas where review, dialogue, or capacity support could be valuable, based on patterns found in the available domestic legal corpus.

## 2. Entry Point 1: National IHR Authority and National IHR Focal Point

**Anchoring pattern:** Relatively stronger than most areas. Mexico's 2025 Reglamento Interior SS (Art. 35 frac. XIX) explicitly designates the Dirección General de Epidemiología (DGE) as the IHR National Focal Point. NOM-017-SSA2-2012 reinforces this designation at the regulatory level.

**Where further review could add value:**

A possible entry point is a review of whether Mexico's current NFP designation — centred on DGE within the Secretaría de Salud — is sufficient to fulfil the distinct National IHR Authority functions introduced by IHR 2024 amendments (Arts. 1 and 4). The 2024 amendments establish the National IHR Authority as a broader, potentially inter-ministerial body with coordination functions across sectors, distinct from the narrower liaison role of the IHR National Focal Point. It remains to be assessed whether the existing DGE designation can be adapted to cover these expanded functions, or whether a formal designation of a broader institutional mechanism would be required.

Further legal and institutional review could focus on: the operational protocols underpinning DGE's NFP functions (24-hour availability, WHO communication procedures, backup coverage); the coordination architecture between DGE, DGRI-SS, and SRE for international health communications; and whether these functions are sufficiently codified in administrative instruments to ensure continuity across changes of administration.

Capacity-building could support: mapping the coordination requirements of the National IHR Authority concept against the current inter-ministerial architecture; reviewing whether an administrative circular or formal institutional instrument would strengthen continuity; and identifying alignment with the national pandemic coordination mechanism contemplated under Pandemic Agreement Art. 15(2).

## 3. Entry Point 2: Surveillance and the Federal-State Implementation Chain

**Anchoring pattern:** Partial. The SINAVE legal framework (LGS Art. 134) and NOM-017-SSA2-2012 provide a nationally established surveillance architecture. DGE's operational authority is anchored in the Reglamento Interior SS. No domestic provision, however, was identified requiring a formal IHR Annex 1A self-assessment, establishing time-bound capacity development plans, or operationally specifying the federal-to-state translation of IHR minimum capacity benchmarks.

**Where further review could add value:**

The preliminary mapping suggests the value of reviewing whether the SINAVE legal framework and NOM-017 are adequate to anchor the expanded IHR 2024 Annex 1 core capacity requirements, which now include community engagement and health services access at the local level, coordination and risk communication and infodemic management at the intermediate level, and clinical guidance provision and health product access at the national level.

A possible entry point is mapping how LGS Art. 13's federal-state competence distribution is operationally translated for IHR implementation. The available corpus does not identify administrative agreements (convenios de coordinación) between the federal Secretaría de Salud and state health secretariats specifically referencing IHR capacity obligations. Reviewing whether such instruments exist, or whether they would strengthen the implementation chain, could be a productive area for dialogue.

Further legal and institutional review could focus on: whether the IHR self-assessment obligation (IHR Annex 1A par. 2) has a domestic procedural equivalent; whether state-level SINAVE nodes are operationally equipped for the expanded local and intermediate capacity requirements in IHR 2024; and whether the notification framework (LGS Art. 136, RLGS Sanidad Internacional Art. 14, Annex 2 decision instrument) reflects the updated IHR 2024 criteria including clusters of severe acute respiratory disease.

Capacity-building could support: developing a federal-state IHR capacity review framework; aligning state-level epidemiological surveillance systems with IHR 2024 Annex 1 requirements; and supporting the IHR Annex 2 risk assessment tooling within DGE operational practice.

## 4. Entry Point 3: Points of Entry, Travellers, and Rights Safeguards

**Anchoring pattern:** Moderate at the regulatory level for PoE designation and minimum capacities; partial to weak for traveller rights, health measure procedures, and ship sanitation certification.

**Where further review could add value:**

The principal regulatory instrument governing international sanitary control at Mexican points of entry — the Reglamento de la LGS en Materia de Sanidad Internacional — dates from 1985. It has not been formally updated to align with IHR 2005, which has been in force for Mexico since 2007. This instrument anchors the largest single share of mapping rows in the PoE, traveller rights, and health documentation areas. The preliminary mapping suggests the value of a systematic review of this regulation against the current IHR text and the IHR 2024 amendments, which extend health measure obligations to embarkation and disembarkation phases and add laboratory capacity to PHEIC-response PoE requirements.

A possible entry point is the procedural and rights-safeguard dimension of health measures applied to travellers. The mapping identified a consistent gap between the constitutional rights protections available under CPEUM Arts. 1, 14, and 29, and the operationalisation of those protections in health-sector implementing provisions. Areas such as informed consent prior to prophylaxis, information provision to travellers subject to health measures, non-discrimination standards, and dignity requirements are not specifically addressed in the implementing regulations identified in the corpus.

Further legal and institutional review could focus on: whether COFEPRIS and SS authorities at designated PoE have codified inter-agency coordination protocols; whether the RLGS Sanidad Internacional Art. 10 designated PoE list is current; whether Ship Sanitation Certificate issuance procedures are codified and the competent authority identified; and whether IHR 2024's digital documentation authorisation (Art. 35) and renamed Maritime Ship Declaration of Health are reflected in PoE operational procedures.

Capacity-building could support: a regulatory review of the 1985 Reglamento for IHR 2005/2024 alignment; development of inter-agency PoE coordination protocols; rights-safeguard guidance for health measure application; and training of competent authority personnel at designated PoE.

## 5. Entry Point 4: Equitable Access and Relevant Health Products

**Anchoring pattern:** No specific anchoring identified in the IHR 2005 mapping. This area is substantially shaped by IHR 2024 amendments and the Pandemic Agreement and is entirely new relative to IHR 2005 corpus coverage.

**Where further review could add value:**

IHR 2024 introduces equitable access to relevant health products as a substantive element of the IHR framework: the IHR purpose (Art. 2) now includes equitable access; Art. 13(8) activates State Party collaboration for equitable access upon a pandemic emergency determination; Arts. 15 and 16 expand the scope of temporary and standing recommendations to include relevant health products. A new definition of "relevant health products" — deliberately broad and including medicines, vaccines, diagnostics, medical devices, personal protective equipment, and cell- and gene-based therapies — is introduced.

A possible entry point is a review of Mexico's emergency use authorisation and regulatory fast-track frameworks (primarily under COFEPRIS) against the expanded "relevant health products" definition, to identify whether all product categories would be covered under emergency regulatory pathways. A further entry point is the domestic procurement and supply chain legal framework, which may warrant review for adequacy under IHR Art. 13(8) pandemic emergency equitable access obligations.

The Pandemic Agreement adds depth to this area through its technology transfer provisions (PA Arts. 10–11), TRIPS flexibility affirmations, supply chain obligations (PA Art. 13), and PABS system (PA Art. 12). The preliminary mapping suggests the value of identifying Mexico's existing technology transfer and local production capacity legal framework before the Agreement's implementation obligations take effect.

Capacity-building could support: mapping COFEPRIS regulatory pathways against the IHR 2024 "relevant health products" definition; reviewing domestic procurement legislation for pandemic emergency scenarios; assessing technology transfer and licencing frameworks under PA Arts. 10–11; and preparing a pre-ratification implementation gap review for the Pandemic Agreement supply chain and access provisions.

## 6. Entry Point 5: Planning, Budget, and Sustainable Capacities

**Anchoring pattern:** Indirect contextual anchoring only. LFPRH and Ley de Planeación provide the general budget and planning framework but no domestic provision specifically designates sustainable financing for IHR core capacity development or establishes a dedicated IHR implementation budget mechanism.

**Where further review could add value:**

IHR 2024 introduces a substantive new financing obligation: under the amended Art. 44, States Parties are expected to provide sustainable domestic funding for IHR implementation. A Coordinating Financial Mechanism is established (Art. 44 bis) to support developing countries. The IHR functioning review is required to include financing. These are new domestic-facing obligations without identified anchoring in the current corpus.

A possible entry point is connecting IHR 2024 sustainable financing obligations to Mexico's national planning cycle. The Ley de Planeación (Art. 21 par. 4) contains a provision that IHR-type international obligations should be reflected in planning instruments; the preliminary mapping flagged this as an indirect anchor. Reviewing how IHR implementation priorities are or could be reflected in the Plan Nacional de Desarrollo and in sector-specific health planning instruments could be a productive institutional dialogue point.

Further legal and institutional review could focus on: whether the Presupuesto de Egresos de la Federación has or requires a dedicated line for IHR core capacity development; whether ASF's audit scope covers IHR-related health spending as a distinct reviewable category; and whether Mexico's participation in the IHR Coordinating Financial Mechanism and the Pandemic Agreement's equivalent mechanism (PA Art. 18) requires domestic legislative enabling.

Capacity-building could support: developing a budget tracking and expenditure review framework for IHR and Pandemic Agreement implementation; connecting IHR capacity development to existing health sector planning cycles; and preparing for engagement with the IHR Coordinating Financial Mechanism.

## 7. Entry Point 6: Parliamentary and Oversight Mechanisms

**Anchoring pattern:** Indirect, through general constitutional functions. The Senado approves international treaties under CPEUM Art. 76 frac. I. The Cámara de Diputados controls the health budget. ASF audits health sector expenditure. CNDH has advisory and investigatory functions for rights compliance. None of these functions are specifically configured for IHR or Pandemic Agreement monitoring in the available corpus.

**Where further review could add value:**

A possible entry point is identifying whether existing Senate or congressional committees have a standing function for review of IHR and Pandemic Agreement implementation, or whether one could be established or activated. Parliamentary engagement in IHR implementation oversight — including through budget scrutiny, executive reporting obligations, and ratification processes — is particularly relevant given the new IHR 2024 States Parties Committee for Implementation (Art. 54 bis), which will require State Party engagement and domestic reporting linkages.

A parliamentary or administrative review could clarify: the reporting chain from the executive (DGE/DGRI/SS) to the legislature on IHR implementation progress; whether existing ASF audit categories cover IHR core capacity spending; how CNDH and INAI can contribute to rights-safeguard and data protection oversight in the IHR and Pandemic Agreement implementation context; and what legislative steps would be required before ratification of the Pandemic Agreement.

Further legal and institutional review could focus on: the constitutional procedure for Pandemic Agreement ratification (Senado approval under CPEUM Art. 76 frac. I); whether implementing legislation is needed before or after ratification; and what parliamentary reporting obligations under PA Art. 21 would require domestically.

Capacity-building could support: parliamentary briefing on IHR 2024 and Pandemic Agreement implementation obligations; preparation of a pre-ratification legislative review matrix; identification of ASF audit indicators for IHR capacity spending; and strengthening engagement with the IHR 2024 States Parties Committee for Implementation.

## 8. Entry Point 7: PABS Readiness — Subject to Final Annex

> **Important:** All references to PABS in this section are based on the IGWG Bureau draft PABS Annex dated 9 March 2026. This draft has not been adopted and is not legally binding. All assessments in this section are provisional and must be updated after the final PABS Annex is adopted and its legal status is confirmed.

**Anchoring pattern:** No specific domestic anchoring identified. IHR Art. 46 (biological substances) provides a weak contextual anchor (level 1 only). The Reglamento LGS Investigación Art. 11 addresses international research collaboration involving biological samples in an indirect capacity. No domestic framework for pathogen access and benefit-sharing has been identified in the available corpus.

**Where further review could add value:**

The PABS system, once finalised, will establish obligations for States Parties relating to: access to pathogen materials with pandemic potential and their sequence information; benefit-sharing including through licences, technology transfer, capacity-building, and supply allocation; and governance of the system. PA Art. 12(6) requires Parties to review and, as appropriate, align national and regional pathogen access frameworks with the PABS system.

A possible entry point is mapping whether Mexico has existing domestic legislation governing: the collection, storage, and international transfer of biological samples with pandemic potential; sequence data generation and sharing; and biosafety and biosecurity requirements for pathogen handling. These assessments would require review by specialists in biosafety law, intellectual property, and health regulation, and should be treated as preparatory rather than prescriptive at this stage.

Further legal and institutional review could focus on: the roles of COFEPRIS, InDRE, and SENASICA in pathogen access and benefit-sharing governance; the TRIPS flexibility and technology transfer provisions of Pandemic Agreement Arts. 11–12 and their domestic legal interface; and the data governance implications of sequence information sharing under the PABS system, including intersections with LGPDPPSO and INAI oversight.

Capacity-building could support: a technical workshop on PABS readiness mapping for Mexican laboratory and regulatory authorities; preparation of a legislative review matrix for PABS implementation after final Annex adoption; and assessment of InDRE's and the national laboratory network's capacity relative to PABS system operational requirements.
