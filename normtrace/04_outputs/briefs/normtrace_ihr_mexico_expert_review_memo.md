## What is NormTrace-IHR?

NormTrace-IHR is a methodological framework for mapping the degree to which international health law obligations are anchored — or not — in domestic legal and institutional frameworks. It applies a structured hermeneutic analysis to national legal corpora: not keyword-matching, but a systematic reading of statutory mandates, regulatory instruments, and constitutional provisions for how they operationalise (or fail to operationalise) specific international obligations.

The framework produces structured outputs at four levels: a country snapshot summarising the overall anchoring profile; a machine-readable obligation-to-provision mapping table; a gap map linking anchoring patterns to forward-looking obligations under the IHR 2024 amendments and the WHO Pandemic Agreement; and a capacity-building brief identifying entry points for institutional dialogue and technical assistance.

The pilot country is Mexico, assessed against the 45 domestic anchoring obligations identified from the International Health Regulations 2005. The Mexico corpus comprises 18 federal-level legal instruments, ranging from the Constitution and the Ley General de Salud to the 1985 Reglamento de Sanidad Internacional and the 2025 Reglamento Interior de la Secretaría de Salud.

---

## Why it Matters for IHR 2024 and the Pandemic Agreement

The 2024 IHR amendments and the WHO Pandemic Agreement, once in force, will impose new obligations on States Parties — in surveillance, reporting, health product access, financing, parliamentary oversight, and pathogen access and benefit-sharing. Whether and how States Parties will be able to fulfil those obligations depends, in large part, on what domestic legal architecture they already possess. The literature on IHR implementation has documented the gap between legal ratification and operational implementation (Fidler 2005; Frenk and Moon 2013; Gostin et al. 2016), but granular legal internalisation mapping has remained methodologically underdeveloped.

NormTrace-IHR proposes a replicable, structured methodology to fill that gap. Its purpose is not compliance assessment — it does not determine whether Mexico or any other State Party is meeting its obligations — but legal anchoring diagnosis: a first-pass identification of where domestic legal infrastructure exists, where it is partial or outdated, and where it is absent. Such analysis can inform capacity-building support, legislative technical assistance, pre-ratification impact assessment, and parliamentary briefing, without requiring the methodological and political judgments involved in compliance scoring.

The Mexico pilot is designed to demonstrate the method, test the pipeline, and generate preliminary findings for calibration by experts in global health governance, international health law, and comparative health systems.

## What the Mexico Pilot Does — and Does Not Do

**What the pilot does:**

The pilot maps 45 IHR 2005 obligations to specific provisions in Mexico's federal legal corpus, using a five-dimension anchoring scale (actor fit, procedure fit, coordination fit, enforcement fit, rights-safeguard fit), scores each obligation from 0 (no identifiable anchoring) to 5 (integrated implementation), classifies the gap type where anchoring is incomplete, and links each obligation cluster to the corresponding IHR 2024 and Pandemic Agreement implications. The full mapping table (80 rows, 18 fields) and the gap map (19 areas) are included in the expert review package.

**What the pilot does not do:**

The pilot does not assess, imply, or suggest whether Mexico is compliant or non-compliant with any IHR obligation or other international instrument. It does not evaluate whether legal provisions are in fact applied, enforced, or interpreted in practice. It does not cover state-level legislation, administrative circulars, diplomatic practice, or judicial decisions. It does not assess factual institutional capacity (staffing, infrastructure, funding) beyond what is traceable in the statutory and regulatory corpus. All outputs are marked `preliminary_ai_assisted` and have not been validated by a qualified legal professional. The analysis is therefore a starting point for expert review, not a finished legal product.

## Preliminary Findings

The Mexico pilot yields five preliminary findings that the project team considers substantive enough to warrant expert engagement.

**Finding 1: A statutory architecture exists but operationalisation is incomplete.** Mexico has a substantial federal legal framework relevant to IHR implementation — a designated National Focal Point (DGE, anchored in the 2025 Reglamento Interior SS Art. 35 frac. XIX), a national surveillance system (SINAVE, LGS Art. 134, NOM-017-SSA2), a constitutional public health emergency framework (CPEUM Art. 73 frac. XVI, CSG competence), and a strong data protection regime (LGPDPPSO Art. 16). The average anchoring level across 45 obligations is 1.76/5, indicating the system is closer to administrative-operational anchoring than to statutory codification at the procedural and rights-safeguard level.

**Finding 2: The 1985 Reglamento de Sanidad Internacional is the single most critical gap.** This regulatory instrument — formally entitled the Reglamento de la Ley General de Salud en Materia de Sanidad Internacional — anchors the largest single share of mapping rows covering points of entry, traveller health measures, and health documentation. It has not been formally updated since 1985 and predates the IHR 2005 by twenty years. Its currency for IHR 2005 obligations, and its adequacy for IHR 2024 amendments, requires urgent expert legal review.

**Finding 3: Procedural and rights-safeguard gaps are the dominant gap pattern.** Of the gap types identified, procedural gaps (34% of mapping rows) and rights-safeguard gaps (12%) are the most frequent alongside regulatory gaps (30%). This pattern suggests that the problem is less the absence of legal mandates than the absence of operationalised procedures, time-bound obligations, WHO communication protocols, and rights protections in the implementing instruments. Several constitutional-level rights protections exist (CPEUM Arts. 1, 14, 16, 29) but are not operationalised in health-sector implementing provisions for the specific IHR context.

**Finding 4: The IHR 2024 amendments and Pandemic Agreement introduce obligation clusters with no IHR 2005 baseline anchoring.** Equitable access to relevant health products (IHR 2024 Arts. 2, 13(8), 15, 16; PA Arts. 9–14), sustainable financing for IHR core capacities (IHR 2024 Art. 44 bis), and PABS-related implementation (PA Art. 12) have no identified domestic anchoring in the available corpus. These gaps are forward-looking: they require pre-ratification legal review before implementation obligations take effect.

**Finding 5: Federal-state implementation architecture is analytically opaque at this corpus level.** LGS Art. 13 distributes health competences between federal and state levels, and the SFSS framework (Art. 9o Bis) provides a coordination architecture. However, whether and how IHR obligations are operationally translated to the 32 federal entities is not visible from the federal corpus alone. The federal legal basis exists; the sub-national implementation chain is not mapped.

## Potential Uses

The preliminary mapping suggests potential value for several audiences and institutional purposes.

For **WHO, PAHO, and IPU**, the methodology could provide a replicable tool for pre-ratification legal diagnostics, supplementing JEE and SPAR processes with a domestic legal anchoring dimension that neither instrument currently captures. For **national parliaments and delegations**, the gap map offers a structured basis for legislative review briefings prior to Pandemic Agreement ratification, identifying specific instruments that would require amendment or enactment. For **capacity-building organisations**, the entry points analysis provides a structured set of legal-institutional dialogue anchors that go beyond political commitment to specific statutory and regulatory provisions.

The methodology is designed to be country-neutral and scalable. The Mexico pilot is a test of the pipeline; if the methodology holds, it could be applied to additional pilot countries within a comparative framework, eventually enabling cross-national analysis of IHR legal internalisation patterns.

## Questions for Expert Feedback

The project team would welcome feedback from each reviewer on the following questions:

1. **Methodological validity:** Does the anchoring scale (0–5) adequately capture the relevant dimensions of legal internalisation for IHR purposes? Are there dimensions (e.g., judicial enforceability, subnational implementation, administrative practice) that should be added, separated, or reweighted?

2. **Comparability and usefulness:** Would a standardised legal anchoring diagnostic, applied across a sample of States Parties, generate information that would be useful for IHR implementation support processes — including the new IHR 2024 States Parties Committee for Implementation, JEE follow-up, or Pandemic Agreement implementation review? What would make it more useful?

3. **Mexico-specific findings:** Are the preliminary findings for Mexico — and in particular the identification of the 1985 Reglamento de Sanidad Internacional as the critical gap instrument — consistent with expert knowledge of Mexico's health governance architecture? Are there major domestic instruments, administrative practices, or institutional arrangements that are absent from the analysis?

4. **PABS and forward-looking obligations:** The PABS analysis is explicitly provisional (based on the IGWG Bureau draft of 9 March 2026). What methodological adaptations would be needed to incorporate PABS implementation analysis once the final annex is adopted?

5. **Scalability and institutional fit:** Would the NormTrace-IHR methodology be a candidate for institutional adoption by WHO, PAHO, IPU, or a dedicated research consortium? What conditions — methodological, institutional, or political — would need to be met?
