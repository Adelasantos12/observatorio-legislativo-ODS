## 1. What This Snapshot Shows

This snapshot summarises a preliminary mapping between the 45 domestic anchoring obligations derived from the IHR 2005 and the domestic legal provisions available in the NormTrace-IHR corpus for Mexico. It is produced by an AI-assisted analytical pipeline and has not been reviewed by a qualified legal professional.

**This snapshot does not:** measure compliance with the IHR; constitute legal advice; make definitive findings on the scope or effect of any legal provision; or reflect the position of any government authority.

**This snapshot does:** identify patterns in the available legal corpus that suggest where domestic anchoring of IHR obligations is relatively stronger, partial, or difficult to trace with available sources. The analysis uses a five-dimension anchoring scale (actor / procedure / coordination / enforcement / rights safeguard) scored from 0 (no anchoring) to 5 (integrated implementation). No obligation in this preliminary mapping exceeds level 3.

## 3. Areas of Relatively Stronger Anchoring

The available corpus suggests relatively stronger domestic anchoring in the following areas. This does not establish full or verified implementation, but indicates a clearer domestic legal basis than in other areas.

**National IHR Focal Point designation (IHR Art. 4).** Mexico's 2025 Reglamento Interior SS (MEX-017, Art. 35 frac. XIX) explicitly designates the Dirección General de Epidemiología (DGE) as the IHR RSI focal point. NOM-017-SSA2-2012 (numeral 6.6.1–6.6.2) reinforces this at the technical-normative level. The institutional designation is legally traceable; operational procedures for 24-hour availability and WHO communication protocols are not fully codified in the corpus.

**Notification of public health events (IHR Art. 6).** Art. 136 LGS establishes a general notification obligation for epidemiological events, and Art. 14 of the Reglamento LGS Sanidad Internacional (MEX-016) assigns a specific duty to notify WHO. These provisions together provide moderate anchoring for the IHR notification obligation, though the Annex 2 decision instrument and 24-hour clock are not domestically specified.

**Points-of-entry designation (IHR Art. 20).** Art. 10 of the Reglamento LGS Sanidad Internacional explicitly lists the designated international airports, seaports, and land border crossings for IHR capacity development, providing the most direct domestic implementation of the IHR Art. 20(1) designation requirement. Art. 352 LGS provides the statutory basis.

**Points-of-entry continuous capacities (IHR Annex 1B).** Arts. 26–27 of the Reglamento LGS Sanidad Internacional specify minimum infrastructure, personnel, and equipment requirements for designated PoE health services, providing moderate regulatory anchoring for Annex 1B continuous capacity requirements.

**Personal data protection (IHR Art. 45).** The Ley General de Protección de Datos Personales en Posesión de Sujetos Obligados (MEX-007), particularly Art. 16, provides a specific emergency exception framework for health-related personal data, directly anchoring the IHR Art. 45 personal data protection obligation. Constitutional backing is provided by Art. 16 CPEUM.

## 4. Areas of Partial Anchoring

The following areas show partial anchoring (level 2), meaning relevant statutory or regulatory provisions exist but do not fully operationalise the IHR obligation. Many rely on indirect statutory delegation or regulatory provisions without explicit IHR-specific procedures.

**Epidemiological surveillance capacity (IHR Art. 5, Annex 1A).** Art. 134 LGS and NOM-017-SSA2-2012 establish the SINAVE legal framework and operational governance. The system architecture is legally traceable; time-bound capacity development plans aligned to IHR Annex 1A benchmarks are not identified in the corpus.

**Public health response capacity (IHR Art. 13, Annex 1A).** Arts. 181 and 184 LGS provide the statutory basis for emergency health response, and Art. 43 frac. IX of the Reglamento Interior SS assigns territorial response duties. Anchoring is partial: IHR-specific response capacity benchmarks and coordination protocols are not codified.

**Federal-state coordination for surveillance and response.** Art. 13 LGS distributes competences between federal and state levels, and Art. 9o Bis (SFSS) provides a framework for the national health system coordination. State-level implementation of IHR obligations is not fully specified in the corpus.

**Traveller rights and health measures (IHR Arts. 23–32).** Arts. 139 LGS and Arts. 68–70 of the Reglamento Sanidad Internacional provide partial anchoring for isolation, quarantine, and traveller treatment procedures. Constitutional protections (CPEUM Arts. 1, 14, 29) provide rights-safeguard context, but implementing health-sector provisions do not fully operationalise IHR-specific informed consent, non-discrimination, or dignity standards.

**International collaboration (IHR Art. 44).** Art. 33 of the Reglamento Interior SS assigns DGRI competence for international health coordination, and Art. 28 LOAPF assigns SRE treaty-management functions. Anchoring is institutional and coordination-based; IHR-specific collaboration obligations (surveillance data sharing, technical support, access to medical countermeasures) are not codified.

## 5. Weak, Uncertain, or Review-Sensitive Areas

The following areas present weak anchoring (level 0–1) in the available corpus, or are flagged for human review due to low confidence or identified legal silence.

- **IHR capacity self-assessment (Annex 1A par. 2):** No domestic provision requiring Mexico to formally assess and report IHR core capacities to WHO was identified. This is a full gap in the available corpus.
- **Extension reporting (IHR Art. 5(2)), WHO verification responses (Arts. 9–10), and Art. 8 consultations:** The DGE NFP is the relevant actor, but no domestic procedure for these WHO-facing communication duties was identified. These may depend on administrative practice or diplomatic channels not captured in the corpus.
- **Ship Sanitation Certificates (IHR Arts. 20(2), 36, 39):** No domestic provision governing SSCEC/SSCC issuance, renewal, or port authorisation was identified. The competent authority (COFEPRIS or SS) and applicable procedure require expanded source review.
- **No charges to travellers for health measures (IHR Art. 40):** No domestic provision prohibiting fees for health measures applied to travellers was identified. This is a full gap in the available corpus.
- **Published charge schedule for goods/cargo (IHR Art. 41):** The Ley Aduanera provides context for non-tariff health restrictions on goods (Art. 56), but no published schedule of charges for health measures applied to cargo was identified.
- **Reporting and review of additional health measures (IHR Arts. 43(3), 43(5), 43(6)):** Procedures for notifying WHO of additional measures and conducting periodic reviews are not domestically codified in the corpus. Anchoring is actor-only via the NFP.
- **Biological substances and reagents (IHR Art. 46):** No domestic provision specifically facilitating the import/export of biological substances, reagents, or diagnostic materials for public health response was identified.
- **Rights safeguards in health measures (IHR Arts. 23, 24, 31, 32, 42):** Constitutional protections exist (CPEUM Arts. 1, 14, 16, 29) but health-sector implementing provisions do not operationalise informed consent, dignity standards, or non-discrimination requirements for the specific IHR health measure context.

## 7. Capacity-Building Entry Points

The following entry points are suggested based on the gap patterns identified in this mapping. These are analytical observations, not legal recommendations or reform proposals.

1. **Clarify and verify the legal basis and operational protocols of the National IHR Focal Point (DGE) function**, including 24-hour availability, WHO communication procedures, and backup coverage.
2. **Review how federal epidemiological surveillance architecture (SINAVE) connects with state-level implementation**, particularly whether federal mandates are operationally translated to all 32 entities.
3. **Examine whether points-of-entry health services — especially at ground crossings and ports — have sufficient procedural, infrastructure, and rights safeguard specification** in regulatory instruments or administrative guidelines beyond the 1985 Reglamento Sanidad Internacional.
4. **Assess the currency of the Reglamento LGS en Materia de Sanidad Internacional (1985)** relative to IHR 2005 obligations. This regulation anchors the largest single share of mapping rows (25/78) but has never been formally updated for IHR 2005 alignment.
5. **Identify whether administrative instruments (circulars, protocols, port health guidelines)** fill procedural gaps for ship sanitation certificates, WHO reporting time windows, and additional health measure review cycles.
6. **Connect IHR implementation with planning, budgetary, and oversight mechanisms**, given that the Ley de Planeación and LFPRH provide indirect anchoring for IHR capacity funding but no explicit IHR budget or planning mandate was identified.
7. **Prepare a legislative and administrative review matrix before any Pandemic Agreement ratification or implementation process**, using this mapping as a baseline for identifying which obligations require new or amended domestic instruments.
