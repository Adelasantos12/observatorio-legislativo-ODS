# NormTrace-IHR Mexico Pilot v0.1
## Legal Internalisation Mapping for Pandemic Governance
### Preliminary Expert Review Draft

**Corpus:** 18 federal legal instruments (Mexico), corpus date 2026-05-05
**Mapping:** 80 rows covering 45 IHR 2005 domestic anchoring obligations

## 1. Executive Summary

This report presents the preliminary findings of the NormTrace-IHR Mexico Pilot: a structured mapping of the degree to which the International Health Regulations 2005 (IHR 2005) obligations are anchored in Mexico's federal domestic legal framework. The analysis uses a hermeneutic, provision-level methodology to assign anchoring scores (0–5), classify gap types, and link anchoring patterns to the forward-looking obligations created by the IHR 2024 amendments and the WHO Pandemic Agreement.

The pilot maps 45 IHR 2005 obligations against 18 federal legal instruments, producing 80 mapping rows. The average anchoring level per obligation is 1.76/5. Five obligation clusters show relatively stronger anchoring at level 3. Twenty-six obligation clusters show partial anchoring at level 2. Fourteen show weak or no anchoring at levels 0–1. Two obligations — the IHR core capacity self-assessment and the prohibition on charges to travellers for health measures — have no domestic match identified in the available corpus.

The dominant gap pattern is procedural: Mexico has a statutory architecture that assigns mandates and designates actors, but the corpus does not reveal fully codified procedures, time-bound obligations, WHO communication protocols, or rights-safeguard operationalisation for most IHR obligations. The single most significant structural finding is that the Reglamento de la Ley General de Salud en Materia de Sanidad Internacional (1985) — the instrument that anchors the largest share of the PoE, traveller health, and health documentation mapping rows — has not been formally updated since 1985 and predates the IHR 2005 by two decades.

The IHR 2024 amendments and the Pandemic Agreement introduce obligation clusters — equitable access to relevant health products, sustainable IHR financing, pathogen access and benefit-sharing — for which no baseline domestic anchoring is identified in the current corpus. All findings are preliminary and subject to expert legal validation. No finding constitutes a compliance determination or a recommendation for legislative reform.

## 2. Why Legal Internalisation Matters for IHR 2024 and the Pandemic Agreement

### 2.1 The internalisation gap in international health law

The IHR 2005 created binding obligations for 196 States Parties, but the history of IHR implementation has documented a persistent gap between international legal commitment and domestic legal operationalisation. States Parties may have ratified the IHR without enacting the statutory amendments, regulatory reforms, or institutional designations needed to make those obligations operative in domestic legal orders. This gap reflects the structural complexity of translating international obligations into multi-level domestic frameworks — particularly in federal states where health governance is distributed across constitutional, legislative, regulatory, and administrative levels.

Existing international assessment tools — the Joint External Evaluation (JEE), the State Party Annual Report (SPAR), and the Benchmarks for IHR Capacities — focus on factual and operational capacity and are politically sensitive. None undertakes a provision-level mapping of whether domestic legal texts actually authorise and operationalise the specific obligations that States Parties have committed to. The result is that capacity assessments can show high scores for institutional functions that rest on fragile or outdated legal bases.

### 2.2 The stakes of IHR 2024 and the Pandemic Agreement

The 2024 IHR amendments (WHA77.17, in force 19 July 2025) introduce substantive new obligations in national governance (National IHR Authority), equitable access to relevant health products, sustainable financing (Art. 44 bis), expanded pandemic emergency response, States Parties implementation review (Art. 54 bis), expanded Annex 1 core capacity requirements, digital documentation, and conveyance operator obligations, among others.

The WHO Pandemic Agreement, adopted at WHA78 in May 2025, adds obligations covering prevention, preparedness, detection, response, equitable access, financing, research and development, and implementation review. Its pathogen access and benefit-sharing instrument (PABS Annex) remains under negotiation. Together, these instruments create the most significant expansion of States Parties' international health law obligations since the IHR 2005 entered into force. For States to implement these obligations effectively, a clear diagnosis of what their domestic legal frameworks already provide — and where the gaps lie — is analytically and practically necessary.

### 2.3 The specific challenge for Mexico

Mexico is a federal constitutional state with a complex distribution of health governance competences. Its constitutional framework places the Consejo de Salubridad General in a position of general authority for public health emergency measures, while ordinary health governance is shared between federal and state levels under the Ley General de Salud. The federal legal framework provides the analytical basis for this pilot; state-level implementation — constitutionally and practically critical for several IHR obligations — is not mapped in this version.

## 3. What NormTrace-IHR Measures and Does Not Measure

### 3.1 The anchoring concept

Legal internalisation, as used in NormTrace-IHR, refers to the degree to which an international obligation has a domestic legal basis sufficient to make it operative in the domestic legal order. This is distinct from compliance (whether the obligation is fulfilled), implementation (whether the operational arrangements are in place), and enforcement (whether violations can be sanctioned). NormTrace-IHR focuses on the legal text: does a constitutional, statutory, regulatory, or administrative provision exist that specifically addresses the relevant obligation, designates a competent actor, and establishes or enables the necessary procedure?

### 3.2 The anchoring scale

Obligations are scored 0–5. Level 0: no identifiable anchoring. Level 1: weak contextual anchoring through a general provision not specifically directed at the obligation. Level 2: administrative or partial statutory anchoring through instruments that are indirect, incomplete, or address only part of the obligation's scope. Level 3: partial statutory anchoring — a statutory provision clearly covers the obligation but incompletely in scope, procedure, or rights-safeguard specificity. Levels 4 and 5 (strong and integrated implementation anchoring) are not reached in the Mexico v0.1 mapping. The scoring is conservative: when in doubt between two levels, the lower is assigned.

### 3.3 Gap typology

Where anchoring is incomplete, the nature of the gap is classified across ten gap types (see Technical Annex §1.2). The most frequent gap types in the Mexico pilot are: procedural gap (no procedure defined for how the obligation is carried out despite a statutory mandate), regulatory gap (obligation addressed through administrative instruments without statutory basis), and rights-safeguard gap (the obligation involves rights of persons, but health-sector implementing provisions do not operationalise those rights for the IHR context).

### 3.4 What is not measured

The methodology does not assess: administrative practice; judicial interpretation of domestic provisions; factual institutional capacity; diplomatic practice; state-level legislation; or funding and staffing. Expert reviewers are invited to identify where administrative practice or judicial interpretation would materially change an anchoring assessment.

## 4. Mexico Pilot: Legal-Institutional Architecture

### 4.1 Constitutional framework

Mexico's 1917 Constitution (CPEUM) distributes health governance across several axes. Article 4 establishes the right to health protection as a constitutional right. Article 73, fraction XVI, assigns concurrent federal-state health competence and establishes the Consejo de Salubridad General (CSG) — with direct presidential subordination — as the constitutionally-mandated sanitary authority with authority to declare national health emergencies and issue obligatory health measures. Article 76, fraction I, assigns the Senado de la República exclusive competence for treaty ratification, including the IHR and the Pandemic Agreement. The 2011 constitutional reform incorporating international human rights treaties at constitutional rank (CPEUM Art. 1) is relevant to the rights-safeguard dimension of IHR obligations.

### 4.2 Statutory framework

The Ley General de Salud (LGS, as amended to January 2026) is the primary statutory instrument for health governance. Key provisions for IHR implementation include Article 134 (epidemiological surveillance mandate, SINAVE creation), Articles 139 and 180–184 (health emergency and isolation/quarantine authorities), Article 352 (PoE health services), and Article 146 (laboratory authority). The data protection framework (LGPDPPSO, 2017) provides IHR Art. 45 anchoring. The Ley Federal de Presupuesto y Responsabilidad Hacendaria (LFPRH) and Ley de Planeación provide the budget and planning context for IHR capacity investment.

### 4.3 Regulatory framework

Two regulatory instruments carry the greatest analytical weight. The Reglamento de la Ley General de Salud en Materia de Sanidad Internacional (RLGS Sanidad Internacional, 1985) designates points of entry (Art. 10), specifies minimum PoE health service capacities (Arts. 26–27), assigns WHO notification duties to DGE (Art. 14), and provides the framework for health documentation and traveller health measures (Arts. 68–70). Despite its analytical centrality, it dates from 1985 and has never been formally updated for IHR 2005 alignment. The Reglamento Interior de la Secretaría de Salud 2025 (MEX-017, February 2025) provides, in Article 35 fraction XIX, the most explicit domestic codification of any specific IHR obligation identified in the mapping: the designation of DGE as the RSI/IHR National Focal Point.

NOM-017-SSA2-2012 provides the main technical-normative framework for SINAVE governance, disease notification, and DGE surveillance functions.

### 4.4 Institutional actors

The mapping identifies fourteen institutional actors bearing primary responsibilities for IHR implementation: Secretaría de Salud (primary health authority); DGE (designated IHR NFP, SINAVE operations); Consejo de Salubridad General (constitutional emergency authority); COFEPRIS (sanitary control at PoE); CENAPRECE (emergency response); DGRI-SS (international health coordination); Secretaría de Relaciones Exteriores (treaty management); state health secretariats (subnational implementation); port and airport authorities (PoE infrastructure); INM (traveller processing at PoE); Senado (treaty ratification, legislative oversight); Cámara de Diputados (budget approval); ASF (audit); and CNDH/INAI (rights and data protection oversight). InDRE (national reference laboratory) is referenced in the gap map's laboratory section with an explicit caveat that it was not identified in the current actors file and requires verification.

### 5.1 Overall metrics

| Obligations with no domestic match identified | 2 |
| Mapping rows requiring human review | 13 |

The overall profile indicates that Mexico has a substantial statutory and institutional architecture relevant to IHR implementation — with strong actor fit across most obligation clusters — but that the operational translation of this architecture into codified procedures, time-bound obligations, and rights safeguards is incomplete in the available corpus.

### 5.2 Gap type distribution

Procedural gaps constitute 34% of mapping rows; regulatory gaps 30%; partial regulatory gaps and rights-safeguard gaps 12% each; full gaps and coordination gaps 6% each. The prevalence of procedural gaps reflects a pattern in which statutory mandates and actor designations exist but are not supported by operationally codified procedures in the federal regulatory corpus.

## 6. Main Findings

### 6.1 Relatively stronger anchoring

Five obligation clusters are assessed at anchoring level 3. These areas are described as "relatively stronger" in comparison to the mapping profile; they do not represent complete or operationally robust anchoring.

**National IHR Focal Point and Authority (IHR Art. 4).** Reglamento Interior SS 2025, Art. 35 frac. XIX, explicitly designates DGE as the RSI/IHR focal point. NOM-017-SSA2-2012 reinforces the designation at the technical-normative level. Actor-level anchoring is strong; operational procedures for 24-hour availability and WHO communication protocols are not codified in the corpus.

**Primary event notification (IHR Art. 6(1)).** LGS Art. 136 frac. I and RLGS Sanidad Internacional Art. 14 together provide statutory and regulatory anchoring for the WHO notification obligation. The institutional channel is designated. The Annex 2 risk assessment decision instrument is not domestically codified.

**Points-of-entry designation and continuous capacities (IHR Art. 20, Annex 1B).** RLGS Sanidad Internacional Arts. 10 and 26–27 provide the most specific domestic regulatory framework for PoE-related IHR obligations, including explicit designation of airports, seaports, and land border crossings. The update-review flag on this 1985 regulation applies throughout.

**Personal data protection (IHR Art. 45).** LGPDPPSO Art. 16, supported by CPEUM Art. 16, provides specific statutory anchoring with an emergency exception framework and INAI oversight.

### 6.2 Partial anchoring

The twenty-six obligation clusters at anchoring level 2 reveal a consistent pattern: statutory authority and actor designations exist, but the corpus does not demonstrate fully codified procedures, IHR-specific benchmarks, or complete rights-safeguard operationalisation. The most analytically significant areas at this level are the following.

**Epidemiological surveillance (IHR Art. 5, Annex 1A).** SINAVE (LGS Art. 134, NOM-017) provides a nationally established surveillance architecture. The IHR self-assessment obligation (Annex 1A par. 2) — requiring formal capacity assessment and reporting to WHO — has no domestic procedural equivalent identified in the corpus. This sub-obligation is classified as a full gap within an otherwise partial anchoring area.

**Health measures and travellers (IHR Arts. 23–32).** LGS Art. 139 and RLGS Arts. 68–70 provide statutory and regulatory authority for isolation, quarantine, and traveller procedures. IHR-specific requirements for informed consent, dignity standards, non-discrimination, and least-restrictive-means principles are not operationalised in health-sector implementing provisions identified in the corpus. Constitutional protections exist at the CPEUM level (Arts. 1, 14, 29) but are not translated into sector-specific IHR implementing rules.

**International collaboration (IHR Art. 44).** Institutional mandates for international health collaboration exist (DGRI-SS Art. 33, LOAPF Art. 28). The specific IHR Art. 44 duties — surveillance data sharing, technical support, access to medical countermeasures — are not codified beyond the general institutional mandate level.

### 6.3 Weak, uncertain, or review-sensitive areas

Fourteen obligation clusters show anchoring at level 0 or 1. The analytically most significant full and near-full gaps are the following.

**Full gap — IHR capacity self-assessment (Annex 1A par. 2).** No provision requiring Mexico to formally assess and report IHR core capacities to WHO was identified.

**Full gap — no charges to travellers (IHR Art. 40).** No domestic provision prohibiting fees for health measures applied to travellers under the IHR was identified.

**Actor-only anchoring — verification and WHO communication duties (IHR Arts. 8, 9(2), 10(2)).** These obligations are anchored only through the DGE NFP designation, without any identifiable domestic procedure for response timing, internal authorization, or communication protocols.

**Procedural gap — Ship Sanitation Certificates (IHR Arts. 20(2), 36, 39).** No domestic provision governing SSCEC/SSCC issuance, renewal, recognition, or port authorisation was identified in the corpus.

**Gap — risk communication.** No dedicated risk communication legal framework for public health emergencies was identified. IHR 2024 Annex 1 explicitly adds risk communication and infodemic management as a national-level core capacity requirement.

## 7. Implications for IHR 2024

**National IHR Authority (Art. 4 as amended).** The 2024 amendments introduce a distinct National IHR Authority concept — potentially broader than the National Focal Point — with inter-ministerial coordination functions. Whether the current DGE NFP designation extends to National IHR Authority functions requires expert legal analysis. An administrative instrument explicitly designating the National IHR Authority and its coordination mandate could address this gap without requiring statutory amendment.

**Expanded Annex 1 core capacities.** The 2024 amendments expand minimum capacity requirements at community, intermediate, and national levels, adding risk communication, infodemic management, clinical guidance, and access to health products. Whether NOM-017 will be updated to reflect these requirements is a critical administrative question.

**Equitable access to relevant health products (Art. 13(8) and related provisions).** This is the most significant new domestic legal anchoring question. The COFEPRIS regulatory pathway framework, domestic procurement legislation, and technology transfer frameworks require review for adequacy under Art. 13(8) equitable access duties during a pandemic emergency.

**Sustainable financing (Art. 44 bis).** The new IHR Coordinating Financial Mechanism creates a domestic anchoring question about whether LFPRH and Ley de Planeación instruments can be read as requiring specific IHR capacity investment, or whether dedicated instruments would be needed.

**States Parties Committee for Implementation (Art. 54 bis).** The new review mechanism will require domestic reporting linkages and a designated domestic interlocutor — neither of which is identified in the current corpus.

## 8. Implications for the Pandemic Agreement and PABS Readiness

> **All PABS references are based on the IGWG Bureau draft PABS Annex dated 9 March 2026. Not adopted. Not legally binding. Provisional only.**

The Pandemic Agreement creates domestic legal anchoring implications in several areas for which the IHR 2005 baseline mapping provides a diagnostic starting point. Prevention and preparedness (PA Arts. 4–6) will require multi-hazard national preparedness plans with review cycles. The existing constitutional and LGS emergency framework provides a partial basis; a specific requirement for PA-standard national preparedness plans is not identified. Equitable access, technology transfer, and supply chain obligations (PA Arts. 9–14) require a domestic legal review encompassing COFEPRIS regulatory law, industrial property law, and procurement legislation. The Senado ratification procedure (CPEUM Art. 76 frac. I) is constitutionally clear; the question of whether implementing legislation is required before or after ratification requires expert constitutional and health law analysis.

On PABS: Mexico has no identified domestic legal framework for pathogen access and benefit-sharing. The closest existing provisions are IHR Art. 46 (weakly anchored through RLGS Investigación Art. 11) and the LGS laboratory provisions. Three gap map areas are flagged `pabs_dependency = Yes - provisional only`. The PABS area requires new domestic legal review once the final Annex text is adopted.

## 9. Capacity-Building Entry Points

Seven structured entry points are identified in the gap map and capacity-building brief. These are analytical observations, not compliance requirements or reform proposals.

**EP1 — National IHR Authority architecture.** Review whether the current DGE NFP designation is adequate for IHR 2024 National IHR Authority functions, or whether an administrative designation of a broader inter-ministerial coordination mechanism would be appropriate.

**EP2 — Surveillance and the federal-state implementation chain.** Review how the SINAVE framework translates to state-level implementation, whether the IHR self-assessment obligation has a domestic procedural equivalent, and whether IHR 2024 expanded Annex 1 community and intermediate-level requirements are operationally anchored.

**EP3 — Points of entry, travellers, and rights safeguards.** Undertake a systematic legal review of the 1985 Reglamento de Sanidad Internacional for IHR 2005 and 2024 alignment, including PoE designation currency, ship sanitation procedures, traveller rights operationalisation, and digital documentation authorisation.

**EP4 — Equitable access to relevant health products.** Review COFEPRIS regulatory fast-track pathways, domestic procurement frameworks, and technology transfer instruments against IHR 2024 and Pandemic Agreement access and benefit-sharing obligations.

**EP5 — Planning, budget, and sustainable capacities.** Review whether IHR implementation priorities are or could be reflected in the Plan Nacional de Desarrollo and health sector planning instruments, and whether LFPRH and ASF audit frameworks support IHR capacity financing accountability.

**EP6 — Parliamentary and oversight mechanisms.** Review Senate and congressional committee functions relative to IHR and Pandemic Agreement implementation oversight, executive-to-legislature reporting on IHR progress, and legislative steps required before Pandemic Agreement ratification.

**EP7 — PABS readiness (provisional).** Undertake a preliminary legal review of the domestic enabling framework required for Mexico's participation in the PABS system, once the final PABS Annex text is adopted.

## 10. Methodological Limitations

**Corpus limitations.** The analysis covers 18 federal-level instruments. State-level legislation, administrative circulars, port health operational guidelines, WHO–Mexico communication protocols, and judicial decisions are absent from the corpus. Several expected instruments are not yet included (Ley General de Mejora Regulatoria, Ley de Amparo, Ley Orgánica del Congreso).

**Currency risk.** The Reglamento de Sanidad Internacional (1985) and NOM-017-SSA2-2012 (published 2013) carry the greatest analytical weight and the greatest currency risk. Whether either has been substantively updated by ministerial circular or administrative practice cannot be determined from the current corpus alone.

**AI-assisted analysis.** All anchoring scores, gap classifications, and analytical summaries were produced through an AI-assisted pipeline. All 80 mapping rows carry `review_status = preliminary_ai_assisted`. LLM-assisted legal analysis is subject to errors of omission, misclassification, and misinterpretation. Expert legal review of all assessments is required before any output is cited or used in policy processes.

**Conservative scoring.** The methodology assigns the lower score when in doubt. The anchoring profile may underestimate Mexico's domestic legal capacity in areas where administrative practice or judicial interpretation supplements statutory text.

**No compliance assessment.** The findings describe legal text, not legal reality. The methodology explicitly excludes compliance assessment in all forms.

## 11. Next Steps for Expert Review

Expert comment is invited on five questions. (1) Does the anchoring scale and gap typology adequately capture the relevant dimensions of legal internalisation for IHR purposes, and what dimensions are missing or should be reweighted? (2) Are the preliminary anchoring assessments for Mexico consistent with expert knowledge of the domestic legal and institutional architecture, and which assessments appear most likely to require revision? (3) Do the forward-looking implications identified in the gap map accurately characterise new IHR 2024 and Pandemic Agreement obligations? (4) What observations can be made about domestic legal enabling requirements for PABS participation, and what methodological adaptations would NormTrace-IHR require after the final PABS Annex is adopted? (5) Would a standardised provision-level legal anchoring diagnostic, applied across a sample of States Parties, be analytically and institutionally useful for IHR implementation support processes, and what institutional home would be most appropriate?
