# NormTrace-IHR: a legal-institutional traceability methodology for mapping domestic implementation of the International Health Regulations — a pilot application in Mexico

**Adela Santos**

Postdoctoral Visiting Fellow, Global Health Centre, Graduate Institute of International and Development Studies, Geneva, Switzerland. Funded by the Swiss Government Excellence Scholarship (2025–2026).

*Correspondence:* [adela.santos@graduateinstitute.ch]

---

## Abstract

**Background:** The International Health Regulations (2005) (IHR) impose legally binding obligations on States Parties to develop and maintain core capacities for public health security. Two principal monitoring instruments — the State Party Annual Self-Assessment (SPAR) and the Joint External Evaluation (JEE) — assess reported capacity levels but do not reconstruct, obligation by obligation, the domestic legal-institutional architecture through which IHR requirements are implemented. Core Capacity 1 (CC1: national legislation, policy and financing), the foundational legal basis for all other IHR capacities, remains undercharacterised in existing monitoring frameworks. This paper presents NormTrace-IHR, a legal-institutional traceability methodology for mapping the domestic legal anchoring of IHR obligations, and reports an illustrative pilot application in Mexico.

**Methods:** NormTrace-IHR applies a structured five-stage workflow: (i) classification of international obligations; (ii) assembly and codification of a national legal corpus; (iii) construction of a country-specific legal system profile; (iv) obligation-by-provision mapping using a validated anchoring scale (0–5); and (v) gap typology classification. The Mexico pilot mapped all 45 IHR 2005 obligations against 110 legal provisions extracted from 18 domestic instruments. Corpus structuring and preliminary classification were AI-assisted; all outputs are designated preliminary and require expert validation.

**Results:** The mean per-obligation anchoring score was 1.76 out of 5. Regulatory and indirect statutory match types predominated (29 and 26 of 80 mapping rows, respectively), with direct statutory anchors identified in only 7 rows. Procedural gaps (n=27 rows, 33.8%) and regulatory gaps (n=24 rows, 30.0%) were the most frequent gap types. The primary domestic IHR implementation instrument — the Reglamento de la Ley General de Salud en Materia de Sanidad Internacional (1985) — predates IHR 2005 by twenty years. Of 45 IHR obligations, 20 involve CC1 as a primary or co-primary implementation domain.

**Conclusions:** NormTrace-IHR provides a replicable, corpus-based methodology for reconstructing the legal-institutional architecture of IHR implementation at the obligation level. Applied to Mexico, it identifies specific legal reform entry points not discernible from capacity scores alone. The framework is designed to complement existing IHR monitoring instruments and is adaptable across legal systems through AI-assisted structuring.

**Keywords:** International Health Regulations; legal implementation; Core Capacity 1; legal anchoring; SPAR; JEE; Mexico; global health security; Pandemic Agreement; public health law

## Background

The IHR (2005) impose legally binding obligations on 196 States Parties to develop, strengthen, and maintain core capacities for detecting, assessing, reporting, and responding to public health events of potential international concern [1]. Domestic legal implementation of these obligations is not a procedural formality: without an identified legal authority, a defined procedure, and enforceable institutional competences, IHR functions depend on informal administrative practice, which is vulnerable to institutional change, budget interruption, and reduced accountability over time [2]. Article 4(1) of the IHR obliges each State Party to designate or establish a national IHR focal point (NFP) with defined functions, a clear legal mandate, and 24-hour operational capacity — requirements that presuppose a domestic legal-institutional architecture capable of sustaining those functions independently of any particular government's administrative arrangements.

The IHR Monitoring and Evaluation Framework (IHRMEF) provides four complementary instruments for assessing implementation: the mandatory SPAR, the voluntary JEE, After Action Reviews (AARs), and simulation exercises [3,4,6]. SPAR and JEE use structured indicator sets organised around 13–15 core capacities, scored on ordinal scales, and have contributed substantially to establishing a global baseline of preparedness and enabling inter-country comparisons [5,6]. However, neither instrument was designed to reconstruct, obligation by obligation, the specific domestic statutory provisions, institutional competences, procedural routes, and rights safeguards through which IHR requirements are implemented. Core Capacity 1 (CC1: national legislation, policy and financing) is assessed as an aggregate construct, scored against general criteria rather than traced to particular legal texts, articles, and competence attributions.

The analytical consequences of this gap are measurable. Bishowkarma and colleagues [5], analysing 1,445 indicator-matched paired observations from 93 States Parties between 2016 and 2023, documented persistent disagreement between SPAR and JEE scores in three indicator areas, notably including NFP functions — precisely the capacity that IHR Article 4 grounds in explicit legal designation. While recent editions show improved alignment, the underlying structural concern remains: where domestic legal architecture is not traced, it is not possible to determine whether reported capacity corresponds to legally anchored institutional functions or to de facto administrative performance that may be difficult to sustain. A country may perform an IHR function adequately without a stable legal basis for that function, generating a SPAR score that accurately reflects current performance while overstating legal-institutional resilience.

The significance of CC1 extends beyond procedural legality. In federal systems and multi-level governance structures, weak statutory anchoring creates particular vulnerabilities: functions assigned informally to a national ministry may not bind subnational authorities, may not survive administrative reorganisation, and may not generate the enforcement mechanisms needed to ensure consistency across jurisdictions [7]. As global health governance evolves — with IHR 2024 amendments now in force [2] and the WHO Pandemic Agreement adopted in May 2025 [10] — the legal operationalisation of new obligations at the domestic level raises questions that existing monitoring tools are not designed to answer. No standardised methodology currently exists for mapping, obligation by obligation, which domestic legal instruments and actors currently anchor each IHR requirement, at which level of legal hierarchy, and with what procedural and rights-safeguard specificity.

This paper presents NormTrace-IHR as a proposed solution to this methodological gap. Its aim is twofold: to describe the methodology and its analytical logic, and to report an illustrative pilot application in Mexico. Mexico is a federal state with a multi-tiered normative system, a publicly accessible legal corpus, and prior participation in both SPAR and JEE processes, making it a suitable test case for the framework's initial development. The pilot findings are presented as illustrative and preliminary; they have not been verified by a domestic public health law specialist and should not be interpreted as an assessment of Mexico's IHR compliance or operational capacity.

## Methods

### Overview

NormTrace-IHR is a legal-institutional traceability methodology that maps each IHR obligation to the domestic legal provisions, institutional actors, competences, and procedures that constitute its domestic anchoring. It operates through five sequential analytical stages. The methodology was designed as an open, replicable framework, supported by structured datasets, validation scripts, and documented decision rules.

### Stage 1: Obligation classification

All 45 operative IHR 2005 obligations were extracted from the treaty text and coded for: obligation type (institutional designation, surveillance duty, notification requirement, procedural mandate, rights safeguard, or technical capacity requirement); domestic anchoring requirement (the type of legal authority, procedure, or competence required for implementation); and implementation domain, using the IHR core capacity classification scheme (CC1–CC8, PoE, rights protection). For each obligation, the minimum domestic legal requirement was specified — for example, Article 4(1) requires a legal or regulatory instrument designating the NFP, defining its functions and powers, establishing authority to communicate with WHO, and mandating 24-hour operational capacity. IHR 2024 amendments were coded as a separate update-review layer (57 changes logged); Pandemic Agreement obligations (n=83) and PABS draft provisions (n=38, based on the IGWG Bureau draft of 9 March 2026) were included as forward-looking reference layers, explicitly marked provisional.

### Stage 2: Domestic legal corpus assembly

A national legal corpus was assembled from publicly available official sources. Instrument selection was based on substantive relevance to IHR implementation domains across health governance, public administration, federal-state coordination, data protection, and biosafety. For Mexico, the corpus comprised 18 instruments: 1 constitutional text (Constitución Política de los Estados Unidos Mexicanos, CPEUM), 8 federal or national laws (including the Ley General de Salud, LGS; Ley Orgánica de la Administración Pública Federal; and Ley de Bioseguridad), 3 general laws (including data protection laws), 2 federal regulations, 2 internal regulations (reglamentos interiores), 1 norma oficial mexicana (NOM), and 1 administrative agreement. All instruments were converted to structured Markdown and coded with 26 metadata fields covering instrument type, normative hierarchy, issuing authority, sector, legal function, effective date, and relevance to IHR and Pandemic Agreement obligations. Corpus structuring and metadata assignment were AI-assisted using a large language model (Anthropic Claude, 2025–2026). A total of 110 legal provisions were extracted and individually coded.

### Stage 3: Country legal system profile

Accurate legal mapping requires prior reconstruction of the constitutional and administrative logic through which legal authority is organised and delegated domestically. A country legal system profile — designated the "country legal brain" — was developed for Mexico, encoding nine components: constitutional architecture and fundamental rights; treaty incorporation effect (Articles 133 and 1° CPEUM); legal hierarchy (constitution > ley general/federal > reglamento > norma oficial mexicana > acuerdo); federalism and health competence distribution (Articles 4 and 73 CPEUM); health governance structure (Secretaría de Salud as primary IHR authority; Consejo de Salubridad General as constitutional sanitary authority); public administration and delegation (Ley Orgánica de la Administración Pública Federal); regulatory instrument types; legislative drafting patterns (interpretation of statutory formulas such as *corresponde a*, *son atribuciones de*, *deberá*, *se coordinará*); and oversight and accountability mechanisms. This profile governed all mapping decisions, preventing false positives from terminological coincidence between IHR language and domestic statutory text.

### Stage 4: Anchoring assessment

Each domestic provision was assessed against the IHR obligation it addresses using a six-level anchoring scale (0–5): 0 = no identifiable legal-institutional anchor in the available corpus; 1 = actor mention only (institution present but function not anchored); 2 = indirect anchoring through broad mandate language; 3 = administrative or sub-statutory anchoring (NOM, reglamento, acuerdo); 4 = general statutory anchoring (ley); 5 = specific statutory anchoring with identifiable legal authority, operative framing, and enforceable competences. The scale measures textual legal-institutional anchoring as documented in the available corpus; it does not measure operational performance, enforcement, or compliance with IHR obligations. Six fit dimensions were coded for each mapping row: actor fit (is the competent actor legally identified?), procedure fit (are implementation procedures present?), coordination fit (is a coordination mechanism established?), enforcement fit (are sanctions or accountability mechanisms present?), rights-safeguard fit (are IHR-required individual rights protections present?), and federalism fit (is the federal-to-state mandate chain defined?). Each dimension was scored on a four-point qualitative scale: strong / partial / weak / none identified.

### Stage 5: Gap typology

Each obligation area was classified against a ten-type gap typology: legal silence (no anchor identified), competence ambiguity (overlapping or unclear mandates), administrative-only anchoring (sub-statutory basis only), procedural gap (duties without defined procedures), coordination gap (absent coordination mechanisms), federal implementation gap (incomplete vertical mandate chain), rights-safeguard gap (restrictive powers lacking required protections), oversight gap (absent review or accountability mechanisms), budget/capacity gap (legal duties without resource specification), and update-review needed (existing anchoring predates IHR 2005 or 2024 amendments). Gap types are not mutually exclusive.

### AI-assisted structuring and validation pipeline

AI assistance was used for corpus conversion, metadata coding, provision extraction, country legal system profile construction, preliminary anchoring-level assignment, and gap type classification. The AI model did not function as a legal authority; its role was to systematise large volumes of legal text according to researcher-defined schemas, decision rules, and country-specific legal logic. Technical validation included automated schema verification, referential integrity checks across all cross-referenced IDs, value normalisation audits, and a pre-deployment data quality audit (verdict: PASS_WITH_DOCUMENTED_FINDINGS). A Python-based network analysis pipeline was used to derive a legal-institutional traceability network from the structured datasets [source code available in repository].

### Mexico pilot scope

The pilot covered all 45 IHR 2005 operative obligations. Federal-level instruments only were included in v0.1; state-level legislation (32 entities) was excluded due to scope constraints and represents a known limitation. The mapping period was 2025–2026. Pilot findings are illustrative and require expert validation before any policy application.

## Results

### Legal corpus

The Mexico IHR corpus comprised 18 instruments across five normative tiers (Table 1). Federal and national laws constituted the largest tier by instrument count (n=8), reflecting Mexico's constitutional model of congressional delegation of health competences under Article 73(XVI) CPEUM. A total of 110 legal provisions were extracted and coded at the article and fraction level. Fourteen federal-level institutional actors were identified in the corpus; of these, the Secretaría de Salud held the highest degree of corpus-derived legal-institutional connections in the network analysis (degree=59), consistent with its statutory role as primary IHR national authority under LGS Article 17. The Ley General de Salud (degree=23), the CPEUM (degree=22), and the RLGS-SI (degree=20) were the three most connected legal instruments in the network.

### Anchoring distribution

Across 80 mapping rows for 45 IHR 2005 obligations, the mean per-obligation anchoring score was 1.76/5 (per-obligation average: maximum anchoring level per obligation, averaged across all 45). The distribution was concentrated in the lower portion of the scale: anchoring level 2 (indirect or partial statutory) accounted for 63.8% of mapping rows (n=51); level 1 for 23.8% (n=19); and level 3 for 10.0% (n=8). No obligation received a level 4 or 5 score; two obligations received level 0. Direct statutory anchors — provisions that specifically and operatively address the IHR obligation in question — were identified in only 7 of 80 mapping rows (8.8%). Regulatory and indirect statutory matches predominated (n=29 and n=26 respectively), indicating that Mexico's IHR anchoring rests primarily on general mandate language and sub-statutory instruments rather than on obligation-specific statutory provisions (Table 2).

The five obligations with the highest anchoring scores (level 3) were: NFP designation and authority (IHR Art. 4, score 3.0), domestic data protection applicable to personal health data (IHR Art. 45, score 3.0), disease notification protocols (IHR Arts. 6–7, score 2.3), public health response capacity (IHR Art. 13, score 2.0), and points-of-entry designated capacities (IHR Annex 1B, score 2.0). The lowest scores were recorded for charges to travellers and goods (IHR Arts. 40–41, score 0.5) and biological substance controls (IHR Art. 46, score 1.0).

### Gap typology

Procedural gaps were the most frequent gap classification across mapping rows (n=27, 33.8%), followed by regulatory gaps (n=24, 30.0%), partial regulatory gaps (n=10, 12.5%), and rights-safeguard gaps (n=10, 12.5%). Full gaps — where no domestic provision was identified — appeared in 5 rows (6.3%). These data indicate that Mexico's legal architecture provides a general statutory foundation for health governance but lacks the obligation-specific procedural and regulatory design needed to fully operationalise IHR requirements. The gap profile does not reflect legal silence on health governance; it reflects a mismatch between the granularity that IHR obligations require and the generality of the domestic legal provisions currently anchoring them.

A structurally significant finding concerns the RLGS-SI (Reglamento de la Ley General de Salud en Materia de Sanidad Internacional), published in 1985 and not substantively amended to reflect IHR 2005 or subsequent developments. The RLGS-SI is the most directly IHR-relevant domestic instrument in the corpus — governing sanitary measures at points of entry, traveller health documentation, and international health authority coordination — yet produces anchoring scores of 2–3 rather than 4–5. This is a paradigmatic instance of the *update-review needed* gap type: legal anchoring is nominally present, but the instrument predates the international obligations it is expected to implement by two decades, creating alignment deficits across multiple IHR article clusters.

### CC1 as a structural implementation constraint

Of 45 IHR 2005 obligations, 20 involve CC1 (national legislation, policy and financing) as a primary or co-primary implementation domain. These include NFP designation (Art. 4), surveillance capacity (Art. 5), notification and verification requirements (Arts. 6–10), response capacity (Art. 13), points-of-entry designations (Arts. 19–22), and additional health measures (Arts. 42–43). The finding that CC1 is implicated in nearly half of all IHR obligations confirms that it is not a discrete capacity among others but a cross-cutting legal-institutional prerequisite embedded throughout IHR architecture. Weaknesses in CC1 therefore cascade across the implementation system in ways that aggregate capacity scores cannot capture.

---

### Limitations

Several limitations constrain interpretation of the pilot findings. First, the corpus covers 18 federal-level instruments only; state-level legislation for Mexico's 32 entities is excluded, substantially underestimating the complexity of IHR implementation in a federal system where health competences are shared. Second, all mapping outputs are preliminary_systematic; no anchoring scores or gap classifications have been verified by a qualified public health law specialist. Third, the anchoring scale measures textual legal-institutional anchoring in the available corpus — it does not assess whether anchored functions are implemented, resourced, or enforced in practice. A high anchoring score for a provision that is formally valid but practically dormant would overstate legal-institutional capacity. Fourth, PABS-related data are based on a negotiating draft (IGWG Bureau, 9 March 2026) and carry no legal weight. Fifth, the methodology was developed and tested on a single country; its validity, reliability, and sensitivity to legal system variation require testing across multiple jurisdictions and legal families.

## Conclusions

NormTrace-IHR proposes a replicable, auditable methodology for reconstructing the domestic legal-institutional architecture of IHR implementation, obligation by obligation. The Mexico pilot demonstrates that a structured legal corpus, combined with a country-specific legal system profile and a validated anchoring scale, can produce granular findings — a mean anchoring score of 1.76/5, identification of a 1985 regulation as the primary IHR implementation instrument, predominance of procedural and regulatory gaps — that are not recoverable from aggregate capacity scores. That 20 of 45 IHR obligations implicate CC1 as a primary implementation domain confirms that legal-institutional traceability is not a supplementary analytical exercise but a foundational requirement for understanding IHR implementation resilience.

NormTrace-IHR is not a substitute for SPAR or JEE. Capacity monitoring, external evaluation, and legal traceability serve different analytical purposes and answer different questions. The methodological contribution proposed here is to add an obligation-level legal conversion layer to the existing IHRMEF ecosystem — one that connects the international obligation to the specific domestic legal text, actor, and procedure responsible for its implementation. As States Parties undertake the legislative adaptation required by IHR 2024 amendments and the Pandemic Agreement, a legal-institutional traceability infrastructure is likely to become increasingly relevant to both national reform processes and international monitoring. Validation through expert review, comparative application across legal systems, and integration with existing monitoring frameworks represent the natural next steps for this methodological development.
